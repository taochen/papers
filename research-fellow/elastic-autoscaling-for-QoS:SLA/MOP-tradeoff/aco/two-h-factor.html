Volume 37, Issue 9, September 2010, Pages 1584–1596

Bi-Objective Ant Colony Optimization approach to optimize production and maintenance scheduling

  • A. Berrichia, , , ,
  • F. Yalaouib, ,

Available online 21 December 2009
  • http://dx.doi.org/10.1016/j.cor.2009.11.017, How to Cite or Link Using DOI
  • Permissions & Reprints

Abstract

This paper presents an algorithm based on Ant Colony Optimization paradigm to solve the joint production and maintenance scheduling problem. This approach is developed to deal with the model previously proposed in [3] for the parallel machine case. This model is formulated according to a bi-objective approach to find trade-off solutions between both objectives of production and maintenance. Reliability models are used to take into account the maintenance aspect. To improve the quality of solutions found in our previous study, an algorithm based on Multi-Objective Ant Colony Optimization (MOACO) approach is developed. The goal is to simultaneously determine the best assignment of production tasks to machines as well as preventive maintenance (PM) periods of the production system, satisfying at best both objectives of production and maintenance. The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms two well-known Multi-Objective Genetic Algorithms (MOGAs): SPEA 2 and NSGA II.

Keywords

  • Production scheduling;
  • Preventive maintenance (PM);

1. Introduction

Despite the interdependent relationship between the production scheduling and the maintenance planning, the two activities are generally planned and executed separately in real manufacturing systems. For many years the relationship between production and maintenance has been considered as a conflict in management decision. This situation stills the same because of the lack of communication regarding the scheduling requirements of each function [28]. The conflicts may result in an unsatisfied demand or machine breakdowns if the production and maintenance services do not respect the requirements of each other. Several studies have been devoted to optimize the two functions, scheduling and maintenance, but separately. The majority of studies in scheduling taking into account maintenance adopt the approach called “machine scheduling with availability constraints” where the time interval of PM actions as well as their number are known and fixed in advance. Schmidt [25] and Lee [18] investigated and analyzed this approach in greater detail for different constraints and machine environments. In this approach, the maintenance is always privileged compared to production. A detailed literature review of production scheduling taking into account maintenance can be found in [3].

The maintenance and production services must collaborate to achieve a common goal, that of maximizing system productivity. Therefore, both objectives of maintenance and production must be considered with the same importance level. Hence, a solution of the joint production and maintenance problem must be a trade-off between the objectives of the two services. In addition, the ultimate goal of the manager is to have a system which is the most available (or the least unavailable) possible enabling him to execute the production jobs as soon as possible. To reach a common goal and avoid conflicts between the two services, Berrichi et al. [3] have proposed an integrated bi-objective model for parallel machine problem using reliability models to take into consideration the maintenance aspect. Among the various maintenance strategies, we suggested studying the preventive maintenance (PM). In this paper, we propose an algorithm based on Multi-Objective Ant Colony Optimization approach to improve the results obtained in a previous study [3], which allows finding better compromise solutions minimizing simultaneously two important criteria in the manufacturing systems: the makespan and the unavailability of the system. This algorithm will be called PACO (Pareto Ant Colony Optimization). Comparisons are made with two well-known MOGAs: SPEA 2 [31] and NSGA II [6], and the developed algorithm showed a good performance according to several distance metrics. Except an adaptation of NSGA II in [3], literature review shows that no bi-criteria method has been developed to produce a set of trade-off solutions for the problem under consideration. The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 describes the bi-objective model of the joint production and maintenance scheduling problem previously proposed. Section 3 introduces the principles of ACO meta-heuristic, gives a brief literature review and its applications to other fields. Our algorithm according to a Pareto Ant Colony Optimization approach is detailed in Section 4. In Section 5, we compare PACO with two popular MOGAs: NSGA II and SPEA 2. Finally, Section 6 concludes the paper and gives future research.

We suppose that jobs are available at the beginning of the production horizon and preemption is not allowed. 3. Ant Colony Optimization approach

Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) is a population based meta-heuristic, where a colony of artificial ants (simple agents) cooperate to find good solutions to hard combinatorial problems. This method imitates the behavior of ant colonies to solve more complex problems than those solved by the real ants. The artificial ants seek the solutions according to a constructive procedure. Solutions are built in a probabilistic way by taking into account (artificial) pheromone trails which change in a dynamic way to reflect the experience acquired by the agents. The construction of solutions is influenced by the pheromone trails which change with each iteration and a heuristic information depending on the problem to solve. Since the first work of Dorigo et al. [9], several versions of ant based algorithms were developed to solve combinatorial problems such as the traveling salesman problem, the quadratic assignment problem, production scheduling, timetabling, vehicle routing, telecommunications routing and investments planning, among others. Recently, this meta-heuristic is extended to deal with multi-objective optimization problems. Indeed, Multi-Objective Ant Colony Optimization (MOACO) based approach has been used in several fields. Mariano and Morales [19] have developed a MOACO algorithm called Multiple Objective Ant-Q algorithm (MOAQ) to the design of water distribution irrigation networks. Iredi et al. [16] introduced some ACO based techniques and they tested them on a bi-criteria vehicle routing problem. Pareto Ant Colony Optimization (P-ACO), proposed by Doerner et al. [8] was dedicated to solve the multi-objective portfolio selection problem. MACS-VRPTW (Multiple Ant Colony System for Vehicle Routing Problem with Time Windows) algorithm introduced by Gambardella et al. [12] was thought to solve a kind of vehicle routing problems. Doener et al. [7] proposed COMPETants algorithm to deal with bi-objective transportation problem which uses two ant colonies. Gravel et al. [15] developed a MOACO algorithm for a real-world scheduling problem related to the aluminum production industry, called Multiple Objective ACO Meta-heuristic (MOACOM). The ACOAMO algorithm (ACO Approach to Multi-Objective), created by McMullen [20], is dedicated to solve a multi-objective just in time sequencing problem. T’kindt et al. [26] proposed a specific algorithm to solve bi-criteria flow shop scheduling problems. The MOACO approach has been used in other fields like system reliability optimization [29] and resources allocation [5]. Garcia-Martinez et al. [14] gave an overview of MOACO algorithms and presented taxonomy. This taxonomy is based on several criteria: the nature of non-dominated solutions set (Pareto solutions set or just a single solution) as out put, the use of only one or several ant colonies and the association of only one or several heuristic functions. To our knowledge, there is no work using MOACO approach to deal with the joint scheduling of production and maintenance.

4. Proposed MOACO approach

The algorithm PACO developed to solve the model described in Section 2 uses two colonies (families) of ants which mutually collaborate between them: the first colony (see ACO-pm procedure) for maintenance optimization and the second one (see ACO-pro procedure) for production optimization. These colonies share information and collaborate via heuristic information and pheromone trails to build complete efficient solutions to the problem. The global pheromone update rules are applied once all ants have built their complete solutions based on the best non-dominated solutions set.

4.1. Solution encoding

Solutions are composed of two segments: production and maintenance parts. The maintenance part is a series of numbers (integers) which are the theoretical periods of PM actions. The production part is composed of two vectors, a vector containing the numeros of jobs and another one contains the numeros of machines on which the jobs must be executed (Fig. 2). The maintenance periods are uniformly generated in [LB, UB]. LB is the shortest processing time of jobs and UB is a function of processing times of jobs and the number of machines. Fig. 2 shows an example of 3 machines and 8 jobs. The PM periods are 16, 20 and 22 units of time, respectively. The job no. 5 will be assigned to machine no. 2, job no. 4 on machine no. 1, and so on. A PM action on M1 should be done every 16 units of time, on M2 every 20 units and on M3 every 22 units.

4.2. Solution construction

4.2.1. Solution construction of the maintenance part

To find best combinations of PM periods of machines, the first colony takes inspiration from the ant algorithms proposed in [24]. At the beginning, each machine has a list of possible PM periods (Fig. 3). The number of PM periods is independent from the number of machines. Each ant of the colony builds a tour (PM solution) while moving from a list to another one so as to all visit them. In order to better explore the search space, intervention times visited are put in taboo lists to make it possible the other ants to choose other periods. At each iteration, each ant achieves a tour which is a vector containing the intervention periods. Fig. 3 gives an example with four machines and two solutions obtained during an iteration. The first ant visits time no. 3 (of list 1) as PM period of the first machine, time no. 5 (of list 2) as PM period of the second machine and so on.

4.2.1.2. Local pheromone update for the maintenance part

The pheromone quantity τik deposited by ants on node (i, k) at each iteration is updated to reflect the experience acquired by ants. At the beginning, each node (i, k) is initialized with weak amount of pheromone (τik=τ0). During solution construction, the pheromone trails of a node (i, k) visited by an ant is locally updated using the following equation:

(4.4)
τik←(1−ρmlocal)·τik+τ0
ρmlocal is the local pheromone evaporation rate (0<ρmlocal<1) used to avoid unlimited accumulation of pheromone and in some sense to forget bad solutions. Thus, local pheromone updating is applied to explore new areas in the search space. More precisely, the effect of the local updating rule is that each time an ant uses a node (i, k) its pheromone trail τik is reduced, so that the node becomes less attractive for the following ants and helps to avoid that ants follow the same path. In other words, this allows an increase in the exploration of nodes that have not been visited yet and, in practice, has the effect that the algorithm does not show stagnation behavior (i.e., ants do not converge to the generation of a common path) [10].

In addition to the number of iterations (or cycles) and the number of ants, the complexity of most ACO algorithms depends on the manner of calculating the heuristic information. In fact, given the constructive nature of ACO approaches, heuristic information is calculated for all possible moves and for all ants. This can significantly reduce the performance of ACO algorithms if the heuristic function is not chosen wisely. The heuristic information can be calculated a priori or a posteriori [14]. In the first case, the heuristic information is calculated at the beginning and remains unchanged during the execution of the algorithm. In the second case, the heuristic information is dynamic and depends on the current state of the ant. In designing heuristic information, two aspects must be taken into account: the effectiveness in terms of computation and quality of information. Implementations with a priori heuristic information, are satisfying in computing time, but not in the selection of appropriate move of ants. On the contrary, in case of implementations with dynamic heuristic information, we have a precise estimate of the usefulness of each move to the detriment of computation time. In our implementation, we used a priori heuristic information for the maintenance and we have adopted dynamic heuristic information with reasonable computation time for the production part. Indeed, ants make their decisions in two stages (select a machine first then assign a task to that machine).

The complexity of PACO algorithm is O(NC·Na·m(Na+n2)) if the stopping condition is Nc iterations (Na is the number of ants in the colony, m is the number of machines and n is the number of production jobs). Indeed, the complexity of ACO-pm procedure, which is governed by the calculation of heuristic information, is O(m·Na) because heuristic information and pheromone trails are stored in matrices of dimension m·Na. The complexity of ACO-pro procedure, which is also dominated by the calculation of heuristic information, is O(m·n2) since ant selects a machine then assign a job (among the jobs not yet scheduled) until all jobs are assigned. In addition to these two procedures, PACO uses the ranking procedure proposed in [6]. This procedure partitions a population of individuals into subsets (fronts) and assigns a rank for each front. This rank is used as a measure of fitness of individuals. The non-dominated individuals of the whole population have a rank equal to one (the best). 5.2. Test problems and setups

As known, the parameter values of an algorithm greatly affect the quality of solutions. To find good values for the parameters of PACO, we selected and tested a set of potential values for each parameter. Then, intensive experiments were conducted by varying one parameter at each step as in [4], using a trial and error approach. The criterion used to measure the quality of obtained solutions is the C metric. For each test problem the averages of C (PACO, SPEA 2) and C (SPEA 2, PACO) are calculated over the 20 runs, then the average over the 10 test problems is recalculated for each of these two measures and finally the value of the parameter which gives the largest ratio between the average values of C (PACO, SPEA 2) and C (SPEA 2, PACO) is taken as the default value of the parameter. Each algorithm is applied to each test problem 20 times (i.e, 20 runs) as in [2], [17] and [21] when the authors compared their algorithms to NSGA-II or SPEA 2. For each run, a new instance of processing times is generated. We have used the Mann–Whitney U test to see if there exist significant differences between the results obtained by two algorithms for a certain metric M. The Mann–Whitney U test is a non-parametric test to determine whether two samples of observations S1 and S2 come from the same distribution. The null hypothesis is that there is no significant differences between the two samples (they come from a single population) and thus their probability distributions are equal. The alternative hypothesis is that the values of the metric M in sample S1 are higher than those in sample S2. The conditions to its application are the independence of the two samples and the type of observations must be ordinal or continuous.

6. Conclusions and perspective

This paper presented an algorithm based on MOACO approach to assess near-optimal Pareto solution sets for the maintenance and production scheduling problem. We found the PACO algorithm is very performing compared not only to the results obtained in [3], but also compared to another well-known MOGA (SPEA 2). PACO uses two colonies of ants, one for the maintenance part and another one for the production part. The two colonies collaborate between them via suitable and efficient heuristic information and share the knowledge acquired from the complete non-dominated solutions obtained at each iteration. Apart an application of NSGA II algorithm in [3], no bi-objective algorithm has been developed to produce trade-off solutions to the maintenance and production scheduling problem. Moreover, obtained results encourage us to study more complex systems using MOACO by considering other strategies of maintenance, system structures and scheduling constraints. It seems also interesting to develop appropriate local search heuristics for PACO and make comparisons with hybrid MOGAs.
